Andreas,

The limit on the number of iterations (as well as the test on the ratio of two consecutive corrections) are there because we do not do any globalization for the Newton method. Too many iterations are a sign that the predictor value may be outside the basin of attraction. Reducing the step size will provide a better initial iterate (a new predicted value). Limiting the number of iterations and maybe forcing a step-size reduction (before reducing the step size, the solver looks if the Jacobian is out-of-date and first attempts to resolve the problem by updating it) is done for efficiency since each iteration implies an additional function evaluation.

The desired accuracy of the solution is ensured by the tolerance with which the nonlinear system is solved. The stopping test - done on the correction and not on the residual - is related to the ensuing error test (on the local truncation error, LTE). Using asymptotic relations for the LTE of both the predictor and corrector, one can show that the error test can be written in terms of the predictor-corrector difference (that is the accumulated correction over all nonlinear iterations, since the initial starting point for the Newton iterations is the predicted value). This gives the tolerance with which the nonlinear system must be solved. The extra safety coefficients take into account the fact that the actual errors do not necessarily have the assumed asymptotic behavior.

--Radu

Andreas Nicolai wrote: 
Hi there,

while trying to understand the process of error control for the time integration, I found a line in the documentation of CVODE saying that "We allow at most 3 iterations (but this limit can be changed by the user)." right after introducing the convergence criterion.

Now, before discussing this further, I have a general question about how CVODE controls the error in time. For my previous hand-written 1st order implicit Euler scheme, I found the following behaviour: As long as the solution did not diverge, at some point (even after many iterations) and sometimes for a time step that was much too large at this step, the solution converged and I could continue with integration in time (thanks to the unconditionally stable implicit scheme). However, when choosing too large time steps, the errors in the solution were also pretty big (sometimes even "jumping" and ignoring important effects in the solution).

My solution was to limit the number of allowed iterations, and with a Newton scheme I had typically a limit of 10 iterations until convergence - if more were needed, I had to reduce my time step and try again.

Is that in principle the same procedure as used in CVODE? If the number of iterations is bigger than a certain limit, the time step must be too large and is reduced? Or is there a different reason for the iteration limit?

Also, does this "arbitrary" iteration limit actually guarantee that the error term for the time discretization stays below a certain value or is this also controled elsewhere?
