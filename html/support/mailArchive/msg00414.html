Radu,

the jumps in the boundary conditions are typically not a big deal and I cannot 
really avoid them, here's an example of a simple heat conduction boundary 
condition, that I use in my model:

j_T = alpha * (T_e - T_b)

T_b are temperatures from climatic data files and those are typically available 
as hourly recorded data. I typically smooth out these values by lineraly 
interpolating between the values, however, that's not always possible. Also in 
my solver you can specify a start or stop time for a boundary condition 
(essentially a time point to set alpha = 0) which leads to a sudden change in 
boundary fluxes. A typical example for this is an experiment, where you take a 
material, oven-dry it and suddendly drop it into water. The water contact 
boundary condition is then added instantaneously.

This would normally require a restart of the solver, but so far I have been 
lazy and the solver was smart enough to detect these changes, reduce the time 
steps accordingly and my solution matched the expected benchmark results. I 
guess at this point compliments are appropriate, because the CVODE solver is 
indeed very robust and versatile to even allow such grave misuse :-)
