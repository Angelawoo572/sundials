Hi Lutz,

Here's the derivation for FLC BDF of order q in 10 easy steps. Suppose we have computed y_j \approx y(t_j), j = 1,2,....,n-1. Let the past step sizes be h_j = t_j - t_{j-1}, j=1,2,...,n-1 nad h_n = t_n - t_{n-1} be the current step size. We wish to compute y_n \approx y(t_n).

1) Build a predictor polynomial \omega_p of order q that satisfies the following (q+1) conditions:
\omega_p(t_{n-i}) = y_{n-i}, i=1,...,q
\dot \omega_p(t_{n-i}) = \dot y_{n-1}
In other words, \omega_p interpolates the past q computed values plus the derivative at the last computed step. (This answers your second question: \omega_p is build such that it satisfies the condition that its derivative is the computed \dot y at t_{n-1}).

2) Build a corrector polynomial \omega_c of order q that satisfies the following (q+1) conditions:
\omega_c(t_n) = y_n
\omega_c(t_n - i*h_n) = \omega_p(t_n - i*h_n), i=1,...,q
In other words, \omega_c passes through the (unknown at this point) solution at t_n and coincides with the predictor polynomial at q past equidistant points (separated by the current step size h_n).

3) It is easy to see that the q-th order FLC BDF formula can be written simply as
\dot y_n = \omega_c(t_n)
(This answers your first question: this is why we have to evaluate everything at t_n. See the rest of the derivation)

4) Since both \omega_p and \omega_c are q-th order polynomial, let's related them through a new polynomial c(t) (which we will define later) as
\omega_c(t) = \omega_p(t) + c(t) * [ \omega_c(t_n) - \omega_p(t_n) ]

It's obvious that, once plugged into the ODE, the above BDF formula will lead to a nonlinear equation in the desired solution y_n. We will use some form of iteration to solve this nonlinear equation for y at t_n (recall that this is, by construction, \omega_c(t_n) ) and we will use the predicted value at t_n as our initial guess.

5) Denote then
y_{n(0)} = \omega_p(t_n)
\dot y_{n(0)} = \dot \omega_p(t_n)
Note that y_{n(m)}, m=1.2,... will be successive iterates of the nonlinear solver (and y_n will be y_{n(M)} where M is the iteration at which we declare convergence).

6) With the above definitions we have
\omega_c(t) = \omega_p(t) + c(t) * [ y_n - y_{n(0)} ]

7) Now use the relations (1) and (2) to derive conditions on c(t). in other words, evaluate (6) at t_n - i * h_n, i=0,1,...,q and use (1) and (2) to get:
c(t_n) = 1
c(t_n - h_n) = c(t_n - 2*h_n) = ... = c(t_n - q*h_n) = 0
which are (q+1) conditions uniquely defining the q-th order polynomial c(t).

8) Using the relation (6), the BDF formula in (3) can now be written as:
\dot y_n = \dot \omega_p(t_n) + \dot c(t_n) * [ y_n - y_{n(0)} ]
or, using (5)
\dot y_n = \dot y_{n(0)} + \dot c(t_n) * [ y_n - y_{n(0)} ]

All we need to do now to complete the derivation of the BDF formula is find the derivative of c(t) at t_n.

9) Given the conditions (7), i.e. the q roots of the q-th order polynomial c(t), we have:
c(t) = K \Pi_{i=1}^q { [ t - ( t_n - i*h_n ) ] }
And at t=t_n, we get
1 = c(t_n) = K * h_n * (2*h_n) * ... * (q*h_n)
Therefore K = \frac{1}{q!h_n^q} and c(t) is completely determined.

10) Differentiate (9) and evaluate at t=t_n to get
\dot c(t_n) = \frac{1}{h_n} [ 1/1 + 1/2 + ... + 1/q ]
or
h_n \dot c(t_n) = \frac{1}{\beta_0}, where we define \beta_0 = \frac{1}{1+1/2+...+1/q}


All done. Multiply (8) by h_n and use (10) to get the BDF formula as:
h_n \dot y_n = h_n \dot y_{n(0)} + \frac{1}{\beta_0} * [ y_n - y_{n(0)} ]
or
y_n = h_n * \beta_0 * \dot y_n + [ y_{n(0)}  - h_n * \beta_0 * \dot y_{n(0)} ]
or
y_n - y_{n(0)} = h_n * \beta_0 * [\dot y_n - \dot y_{n(0)} ]


Hope that this clarifies it :-)

--Radu

Hello Radu,

I have a question concerning the corrector equation. In the following I use the
same symbols as in your paper "The PVODE and IDA Algorithms" by Alan Hindmarsh.
For a fixed-leading coefficient implementation of BDF equation the predictor
polynomial interpolates (Newton) the calculated y values (past grid values)
taking into account, that the step width varies. That means, the y values used
to determine the predictor polynomial coeficients can be non-equidistant.
In contrast, the corrector polynomial is based on equidistant y values, which
are calculated from the past grid values. The corrector equation is said to be:

alpha_s(y_n-y_n(0))+h_n(y_n_dot-y_n(0)_dot).

The derivation of the above equation is what I am interested in. What is
normally done to explain the derivation is, that for the corrector polynomial
omega_c and the predictor poynomial omega_p the following approach is made:

omega_c(t)=omega_p(t)+c(t)(y_n-y_n(0)).

Next this equation is derived and "evaluated for tn". Can you please explain me
this "evaluation for tn". How do I come from c_dot(tn) to 1/(h_n*Beta_0)?

Closely linked with this problem is another (very small) question. For the
predictor polynomial it is assumed that omega_p_dot(t_(n-1))=y_dot_(n-1). I
wonder how this is fulfilled by the Newton interploation done in the above
mentioned paper.

Could you please help me?

Thank you very much in advance.
