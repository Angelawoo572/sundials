> Comments interspersed below...
> 
> >> I think it makes sense if you can get the truncation error of high
> >> order, which is not the case with BDF and RK methods.
> >> If you need high-precision numerical solutions, and your 
> >> problem is not large, I would consider Taylor series based
> >> methods, where order 30, for example, is not unusual.
> 
> Ned, the floating-point precision has nothing to do with the
> integration method. You confuse truncation and roundoff errors. 

Well, I have been bounding them rigorously in my methods for years.

> can get the errors down to whatever level you want with an adaptive
> BDF (or RK) solver by controlling the stepsize. 

Sure, but the stepsizes will be generally very small. 

> Conversely, th tolerance with which you can solve using a Taylor series
> based method of any order will be limited by the floating-point precision.

Taylor series methods easily allow larger stepsizes at stringent
tolerances. Overall, they can be more efficient than standard methods
when it comes high-precision computations. A good reference is for
example http://www.maia.ub.es/~angel/taylor/ 
Of course, they are not suitable for very stiff ODEs. 

Hope this is helpful.
Ned
