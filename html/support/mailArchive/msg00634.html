Hi Andreas,

Ok, this makes sense to me, even though the term "Globalization" is new to me in this respect.

We do not make any attempt to globalize the Newton method with, as an example, line search.

However, about the efficiency: When considering the total number of iterations in a certain integration time interval, wouldn't fewer steps with more iterations actually lead a globally smaller number of iterations? At least, that was the behavior in my simple Euler solvers. I gain performance, but sacrifice accuracy. How does this relate to CVODE?

Yes, more iterations would allow larger step sizes and the global number of iterations may or may not be smaller. This is why the user has the option of changing the default value of 3.

There is a bit more to this, but I don't want to get into too many details... If you look in the user guide at the Newton stopping test (R*||\delta_m||_WRMS<0.1\epsilon), you'll see that it is derived from the local error test (||\Delta_n||_WRMS<\epsilon) through some more approximations and therefore, even if the Newton iterations converge, there still is a chance that the error test will fail (this is why \Delta_n is checked in CVDoErrorTest) and this chance would only increase if allowing many more iterations in the nonlinear solution.

Bottom line is, the global number of iterations may or may not decrease if you increase the maximum number of iterations allowed at any given step. You'll have to experiment with that.

Not sure if I understand this completely. The predictor step is essentially some explicit prediction of the solution variables using the history of the past steps. Now, during the iteration variables approach the converged solution and may be "swinging" around the target values. How do I have to understand the term "accumulated correction"? Is that the absolute difference between a state variable at different iteration levels which is then added up to get the total correction? Or is it simply the difference between predicted and corrected/final value?

It's the latter. See the CVODE documentation. In computing the solution y^n at t_n, the accumulated correction is \Delta_n = y^{n(m)}-y^{n(0)}, with y^{n(m)} being the final iterate computed and y^{n(0)} the predictor. The correction at the k-th iterate is \delta_k = y^{n(k)} - y^{n(k-1)}.

Also, this asymptotic relation for the LTE (is that the residual which asymptotically approaches zero on convergence?) is new to me, I could only imagine plotting the residuals over the iteration levels and find such an asymptotic behavior for the corrector step. But how do you find such an asymptotic behavior for the predictor? Could you recommand a paper/book where I can read up something on this?

Asymptotic in this case means h->0. When you write your integration method in terms of a difference operator, the local truncation error is the residual of that difference operator applied to the exact solution. For linear multistep methods, the LTE always has the form
LTE(t_n) = C_{q+1} h^{q+1} y^{(q+1)}(t_n) + O(h^{q+1})
so, in CVODE's case, that is true for both the predictor and the corrector. Subtracting them, one can therefore estimate the LTE in terms of the predictor-corrector difference (so-called Milne's estimate). The C' in the CVODE user guide is C' = Cc/(Cc-Cp) where Cc is the constant in the LTE for the corrector formula and Cp the one for the predictor formula and so we use: LTE = C' * \Delta_n + O(h^{q+2}) and the error test is then ||LTE||_WRMS<1 or ||\Delta_n||_WRMS<\epsilon, where \epsilon = 1/C'

You can find all of this detailed in the papers that are at the base of the CVODE implementation. I've already mentioned them to you a while ago (see http://www.llnl.gov/casc/sundials/support/mailArchive/msg00056.html)

Also, look-up the book by Ascher and Petzold (ISBN 0-89871-412-5) as a good introduction.
