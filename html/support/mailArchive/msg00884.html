Thanks Andreas,

This is a cool trick. However, what do you do about jacobian calculations for the RHS function f(t,{y})?
The primary variables have now changed, hence, the jacobian now is defined as
[J] = d {f} / d {y},

as opposed to

[J] = d {f} / d{T}

I guess that we could do the following:
[J] = d {f} / d{y} = (d {f} / d{T}) (d {T} / d{y})

But that requires the inversion of an NxN matrix that is obtained by the last term.
What are your comments on this?

Thanks,
Manav



On Feb 22, 2007, at 11:17 AM, Andreas Nicolai wrote:

Hi Manav,

I solve this kind of problem also and here's how I do it:

Define a vector of unknows y(T) that is essentially a vector of the internal energies in your system,
for you simple problem it can be calculated using your heat capacities:

{y}(t,T) = C(t,T) {T}(t,T)

Now your equation to be solved can be redefined as

d{y}/dt = f(t,{y})

where f(t,{y}) is the right hand side of your problem including a temperature calculation. So inside your f() calculation function, simply calculate the temperatures T from the total energies (from the y vector), which should for your problem boil down to deviding the internal energy by the heat capacity, and then continue calculating the divergence using the calculated temperatures.
