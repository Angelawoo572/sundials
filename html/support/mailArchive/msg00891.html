Hi Joerg and Radu,

Thanks very much for your suggestions. I will track down these
references and have a read. FYI, I found some interesting comments in
these papers, perhaps you may have come across them:

V. A. Mousseau
Implicitly balanced solution of the two-phase flow equations coupled to
nonlinear heat conduction
Journal of Computational Physics, Volume 200, Issue 1, 10 October 2004,
Pages 104-132
doi:10.1016/j.jcp.2004.03.009

The method of lines and the advective equation
Michael B. Carver and H.W. Hinds
SIMULATION, 8 1978; vol. 31: pp. 59 - 69.
doi:10.1177/003754977803100205
(complete with mention of solving with analogue computers :-)

and this book:

Patankar, S.V. (1980)
Numerical Heat Transfer and Fluid Flow
ISBN 0891165223

Key points seem to include the fact that the standard two-phase flow
equations are ill-posed and require 'numerical viscosity' to be added
before they will solve. One approach is to use biased upwind estimates
of one-way variables (eg fluid properties). A 'staggered grid' is
another thing that seems to be used. But these comments seem to all be
in the context of explicit and semi-implicit integration engines.

The first paper deals with fully implicit solvers (like IDA), and talks
about using Krylov methods.

Another interesting area is the 'adaptive method of lines', which I
presume is probably beyond what could easily be done with IDA:

Vande Wouwer, Alain (2001)
Adaptive Method of Lines
ISBN 158488231X

Thanks again for the suggestions,

Cheers
JP
